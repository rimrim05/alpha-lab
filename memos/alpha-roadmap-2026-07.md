# Alpha roadmap — residual-reversion improvement research (2026-07-10)

35 proposals from a 7-axis research sweep (signal, regime, portfolio, execution, universe,
literature, overlay), each adversarially vetted (citations spot-checked, code inspected,
overfit accounting). 11 graded strong, 24 plausible. Deduplicated and sequenced below.
Every item keeps the house rules: mechanism first, kill criteria pre-registered before data,
honest trial counts for deflated Sharpe.

## Run these free diagnostics first (no new code path, ~1 afternoon)

1. **Half-life monotonicity test**: on the EXISTING per-signal outcome log, bucket historical
   trades by estimated AR(1) half-life quintile at entry. If per-trade P&L declines with
   half-life and the slowest quintile is ~zero/negative, the kappa screen (item A) is validated
   before writing a line of strategy code.
2. **Book beta regression**: regress current backtest daily P&L on SPY + the 11 sector ETFs.
   If R² < 3% and |beta| < 0.05, kill the beta-hedge overlay (item C) with one hour of work;
   if beta is 0.1-0.2, the overlay is worth up to +0.2-0.5 Sharpe of pure vol reduction.
3. **Turnover cost decomposition**: measure what fraction of gross P&L the band-churn burns at
   10 bps. That number sizes the Garleanu-Pedersen prize (item E) before building it.


## The ranked changes (strong tier, deduplicated)

### A. OU half-life screen + drift-corrected s-score (restores Avellaneda-Lee's own spec)

**Mechanism.** The s-score assumes the cumulative residual is a stationary OU process, but kappa varies enormously across names. A stretched residual on a slow-reverting name is usually not a mispricing; it is a permanent repricing (fundamental news, factor-loading shift), and shorting it is a bet against information. Only residuals whose estimated half-life is much shorter than the holding horizon carry positive expected reversion per unit time. Avellaneda & Lee themselves impose this: they only trade names whose estimated mean-reversion time tau = 1/kappa is less than half the estimation window (< 30 days on a 60d window), precisely because kappa estimated on a 60d window is unreliable when reversion is slow. If the current implementation computes the s-score without the kappa screen, it is trading exactly the names where the OU model is least valid. Secondary fix from the same AR(1) fit: the 'modified s-score' subtracts the drift term (s_mod = s - a/(kappa*sigma_eq)) so momentum drift is not misread as dislocation.

**Change.** In the signal step, fit AR(1) on the 60d cumulative residual per name (already implicitly available): X_t = a + b*X_{t-1} + eps. kappa = -252*ln(b), half-life = ln(2)/(-ln(b)) trading days. Drop names from the tradable set when b >= 1 or half-life > 30 days (AL's own cutoff, pre-registered, not tuned). Compute sigma_eq = std(eps)/sqrt(1-b^2) and use the drift-corrected s-score. ~30 lines of pandas; one vectorized AR(1) per name per day on data already in memory.

**Evidence.** Avellaneda & Lee (2010, Quantitative Finance 10(7)): the kappa > 252/30 filter is in the original paper (https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1153505). Yeo & Papanicolaou (2017, Risk and Decision Analysis 6(4)) show explicitly that screening residuals by mean-reversion speed and fit quality (R^2) controls the dominant risk in this strategy class, mis-estimated reversion (http://math.stanford.edu/~papanico/pubftp/RDA_manuscript.pdf). OLS bias in kappa on short windows is well documented (Marriott-Pope 1954 lineage), which argues for a conservative cutoff rather than trusting point estimates.

**Expected impact.** Highest-confidence proposal here. Removes the left tail (short squeezes on repricing names, longs into deteriorating fundamentals) rather than raising the mean, so expect robust-core Sharpe (the 1.7 number) to improve more than headline Sharpe, plausibly +0.1 to +0.3 net, and a visibly better worst-drawdown. This is the closest thing to a free lunch in the AL framework because it is part of the original spec.

**Kill criteria.** Walk-forward 2018-present, identical universe/costs, single trial: if the half-life-screened book does not beat the unscreened book on deflated Sharpe AND does not reduce max drawdown, revert. Also kill if the screen drops >60% of trade-days (screen too tight for a 60d window to estimate).

**Skeptic's note.** Kappa estimated by AR(1) on 60 observations is extremely noisy and biased (Marriott-Pope), so the screen is a jittery classifier: names will flip in and out of the tradable set day to day, adding turnover cost, and the screen mostly acts as a breadth reducer on an equal-weight ~100-name book whose Sharpe is driven by diversification. Combined with survivorship bias hiding the delisted blowups the screen is designed to avoid, the measured backtest improvement may be statistically indistinguishable from zero over one 2018-present walk-forward: real live benefit, unprovable in this test harness.

### C. Per-position sector-ETF beta hedge (trade the actual residual)

**Mechanism.** The signal is a residual s-score, but the book holds raw stock positions. A long stock position = long residual + beta x long sector ETF; equal-weight dollar-neutrality nets factor exposure only by accident (longs and shorts can cluster in different sectors/betas, and betas are not equal across legs). That residual factor exposure has zero expected return under the model but adds variance; hedging it raises Sharpe mechanically (same expected PnL numerator, smaller vol denominator) and removes the beta-crash failure mode documented for 'dollar-neutral-only' books. Avellaneda & Lee's own paper does exactly this: each stock trade is paired with -beta_i x sector ETF so the position IS the residual. You already estimate the rolling betas; the hedge is a free byproduct.

**Change.** Each day compute net book exposure per sector ETF: h_j = sum over names in sector j of (w_i x beta_i,lagged). Add an overlay position of -h_j in each XL* ETF (SPY for fallback names). ETFs are pennies-wide so cost the overlay at 1-2 bps, not 5-10. Then replace the dollar-neutrality constraint with residual-neutrality (the overlay makes the book both beta-neutral and sector-neutral simultaneously). Also log the current book's realized daily beta first; that number tells you how big the prize is before you build anything.

**Evidence.** Avellaneda & Lee (2010), 'Statistical arbitrage in the US equities market', Quantitative Finance 10(7), https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1153505. Their ETF-based strategy is defined as beta-hedged residual trading, not raw stock dollar-neutral. On the failure mode of dollar-neutral-only: 'Betas and the myth of market neutrality' (Int. J. Forecasting, 2015) https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0169207015001533 and 'Market neutrality and beta crashes' (2024) https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0927539824001117. Sharpe improves under beta-neutrality vs dollar-neutrality, with the honest caveat that hedge-estimation error can worsen extreme days.

**Expected impact.** Depends entirely on how big the current residual factor exposure is. If realized book beta is already ~0 (plausible with 100+ names both sides), gain is small; if |beta| averages 0.1-0.2 (common for reversal books, since longs are recent losers), hedging can cut book vol 10-25% for zero expected-return cost, i.e. +0.2-0.5 Sharpe. Measure first; the diagnostic is one regression of book PnL on SPY/sector returns.

**Kill criteria.** Diagnostic gate before building: regress current backtest daily PnL on SPY and the 11 sector ETFs; if R-squared < 3% and |beta| < 0.05, kill (nothing to hedge, overlay is pure cost). If built: kill unless book vol falls at least 8% with gross PnL change within noise, net of 2 bps ETF overlay costs.

**Skeptic's note.** The prize may be near zero: with 100+ equal-weight names per side, incidental beta/sector netting could leave little residual exposure, in which case the overlay is pure cost, and 60-day OLS betas are noisy enough (per the cited IJF 2016 paper, which recommends ~1yr windows) that the hedge itself injects estimation error and churn, potentially failing exactly on the crash days it exists to protect against. The diagnostic gate is the mitigation; run it before writing any overlay code, and consider a longer beta window for the hedge leg than for the signal.

### B. Fix the live/backtest fill mismatch: MOC execution (bug fix, do immediately)

**Mechanism.** Two mechanisms. (1) Venue: the closing auction is the deepest single liquidity event of the US day (~10-15% of daily volume in S&P 500 names) and for small orders the closing print carries near-zero impact and no bid-ask crossing, vs the open, which has the widest spreads and highest volatility of the session. (2) Consistency: an s-score backtest computed on CLOSE prices whose live fills happen at the OPEN embeds a systematic backtest-vs-live price mismatch (the overnight gap) that flat bps do not capture; MOC fills make the live fill price literally the price series the backtest uses, collapsing implementation shortfall ambiguity to ~0. Note this trades off against Proposal 1 (which exploits the open): the right sequencing is to run the Proposal-1 cohort test FIRST: if gap-conditioning shows no edge, MOC is strictly better execution; if it shows edge, condition at the open and still exit MOC.

**Change.** Live: replace open market orders with MOC orders submitted before the 3:50pm ET cutoff (Alpaca paper supports order class 'cls'). Backtest: fill at t+1 close instead of t+1 open (both columns free in yfinance), keeping skip=1 signal discipline. Start with exits (churn-heavy, no timing edge claimed) and A/B against open entries on the paper book.

**Evidence.** Cboe, 'The Impact Closing Auctions Have on Volumes' (https://www.cboe.com/insights/posts/the-impact-closing-auctions-have-on-volumes/): closing auction ~9-10% of volume in NYSE-listed S&P 500 names, up to 20% on rebalance days; BMLL closing-auction execution analysis (https://www.bmlltech.com/news/market-insight/sounding-the-bell-understanding-the-closing-auction-for-optimal-execution-in-global-equity-markets); Frazzini, Israel & Moskowitz, 'Trading Costs' (2018, https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3229719): $1.7T of live executions show real costs an order of magnitude below academic estimates, ~5-16 bps even at institutional size, so for a small paper book 10 bps/side is conservative and ~2-5 bps effective at the close is defensible.

**Expected impact.** Mostly robustness, some Sharpe: per-side cost realistically drops from ~5-15 bps effective (open) toward ~2-5 bps (close), worth maybe +0.05-0.15 net Sharpe at this turnover, plus it removes the largest unmodeled live/backtest divergence. Evidence on whether the extra ~17.5h of signal age at open vs the prior close costs alpha is genuinely mixed; hence the A/B rather than a blanket switch.

**Kill criteria.** Run open-fill and close-fill backtests side by side (2 pre-registered trials) and 60 days of paper A/B. Kill the MOC switch if close-fill net Sharpe is lower than open-fill net Sharpe by > 0.1, or if live paper MOC fills deviate from official closing prints (they shouldn't; that would indicate an Alpaca simulation artifact worth knowing about anyway).

**Skeptic's note.** The 60-day paper A/B has essentially zero statistical power to detect a 0.05-0.15 Sharpe difference, so the open-vs-close decision will really be made by the backtest comparison, and both backtest legs share the same survivorship-biased yfinance data, so if residual-reversion alpha is concentrated in the t+1 intraday window (open fills capture it, MOC forfeits it), the historical comparison could still misrank the two under live conditions. Two operational sub-risks: Alpaca rejects CLS orders submitted between 3:50pm and 7:00pm ET, so the nightly cron must run outside that window; and Alpaca's paper engine simulates MOC fills rather than routing to the real auction, so the 'live' leg of the A/B validates the simulator, not actual auction execution (the kill criterion at least detects this).

### E. Garleanu-Pedersen partial-adjustment sizing (attack the turnover leak)

**Mechanism.** Costs are linear in turnover; the alpha lost from lagging your ideal position is second-order (quadratic) in tracking error. Garleanu & Pedersen's closed-form result: with predictable returns and trading costs, the optimal policy is to hold a position that moves only a FRACTION of the way toward the current target each day ('aim in front of the target, trade partially toward the aim'). Your stateful bands are a crude binary version; a continuous target with partial adjustment captures the same alpha at materially lower turnover. Notably, the Pelger et al. deep-learning statarb paper attributes much of its net outperformance to learning the trading POLICY (signal-to-position map that internalizes costs), not exotic signals; this is the stealable non-DL piece.

**Change.** Replace enter/exit bands + equal weight with: target_i = -clip(s_i, -2.5, 2.5)/2.5 * w_max for |s_i| > threshold else decay toward 0; then position_today = position_yesterday + lambda * (target - position_yesterday), lambda ~ 0.2-0.4 (tied to estimated reversion speed: faster tau -> larger lambda, per G&P slower alpha decay gets more weight). Add a no-trade buffer: skip rebalance trades smaller than ~10-20% of the position. Keep dollar neutrality by demeaning targets cross-sectionally. All vectorized pandas.

**Evidence.** Garleanu & Pedersen (2013), 'Dynamic Trading with Predictable Returns and Transaction Costs', Journal of Finance 68(6):2309-2340, https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/jofi.12080, working paper https://nbgarleanu.github.io/DynTrad.pdf (superior NET returns vs naive rebalancing, empirically verified on futures). Policy-layer importance: Guijarro-Ordonez, Pelger & Zanotti (2021/2023), 'Deep Learning Statistical Arbitrage', arXiv:2106.04028, published Management Science 2024.

**Expected impact.** This attacks the gap between gross and net directly: at 5-10bps/side and ~100 positions with band-driven churn, turnover cost is likely your single largest PnL leak. Realistic: 30-50% turnover reduction for <10% gross alpha loss, which at your cost assumptions is a material net Sharpe gain (order +0.2 to +0.4) and, importantly, makes the 10bps stress case survivable. Evidence quality is high (JF theory + empirics), and the mechanism is arithmetic, not a market anomaly; the main risk is implementation bugs, not regime change.

**Kill criteria.** Pre-register lambda in {0.25, 0.5} and buffer in {0, 15%} (4 trials, counted in the deflated-Sharpe accounting). On 2023+ holdout at 10bps/side: turnover must drop >= 30% AND net Sharpe must beat the band version. If gross alpha drops > 15%, the sizing map is wrong; kill rather than tune.

**Skeptic's note.** The existing enter/exit bands already function as a no-trade region under the book's linear (flat-bps) cost model, so most of the turnover saving G&P promises may already be captured; the measured improvement over the band baseline could be near zero, making the projected +0.2 to +0.4 net Sharpe gain the weakest claim in the proposal.

### F. Cross-sectional residual-dispersion gate on entries (regime conditioning)

**Mechanism.** The s-score is standardized per name, so |s|=1.25 in a dead-calm market is a tiny dollar dislocation whose expected reversion cannot clear 10-20 bps round-trip costs, while the same s-score in a high-dispersion market is a large dollar mispricing. Gross reversal profits scale with the cross-sectional variance of noninformational order-flow shocks (the thing the strategy harvests), while costs are roughly constant per trade, so net edge is concentrated in high-dispersion regimes. Dai, Medhat, Novy-Marx & Rizova show reversal is stronger and faster among higher-volatility stocks/periods and that volatility+turnover conditioning is what makes reversal survive costs; Hameed & Mian show reversals are stronger after volatile periods.

**Change.** Each day compute the cross-sectional std of 1-day residuals across the panel (already produced by the factor model, zero new data), smooth over 20 days, and rank vs trailing 1y. Rule: when dispersion is below its trailing 40th percentile, raise entry bands to |s|>=1.75 (fewer, deeper entries); when above the 80th percentile, allow the standard 1.25. Exits unchanged. Alternative same-mechanism variant: leave bands alone and scale gross by dispersion percentile; pick ONE variant before testing, not both.

**Evidence.** Dai, Medhat, Novy-Marx, Rizova, 'Reversals and the Returns to Liquidity Provision', NBER w30917 (2023), Financial Analysts Journal 80(2) 2024 (https://www.nber.org/papers/w30917): higher volatility → faster, initially stronger reversals; conditioning is what delivers net-of-cost profitability. Hameed & Mian, 'Industries and Stock Return Reversals', JFQA 50 (2015): intra-industry reversals (their setup ≈ your sector-residual setup) stronger in volatile states.

**Expected impact.** Probably the highest expected net-Sharpe lift of the five (+0.2 to +0.4 plausible) because it attacks the cost line directly: the current book holds ~100+ equal-weight positions every day including in regimes where median expected reversion per trade is below the 10-20 bps hurdle. Cutting the low-dispersion trades should reduce turnover substantially at small gross-alpha cost. Risk of being fooled: dispersion correlates with VIX, so run it jointly with proposal 1 and check it adds beyond VIX scaling; if it is just VIX in disguise, keep only one.

**Kill criteria.** Pre-register: bucket the existing per-signal outcome log by entry-day dispersion tercile. Kill if net P&L per trade is not monotonically increasing in dispersion tercile, or if the gated book's deflated Sharpe (2 trials: gate + threshold choice) does not beat the ungated book net of costs, or if the entire improvement disappears after residualizing dispersion on VIX percentile.

**Skeptic's note.** Regime gates have an effective sample size equal to the number of regime transitions, not days: 2018-present contains only ~3-4 dispersion regimes, so the in-sample lift can come from one calm stretch (2018-19) that was bad for the book for unrelated reasons, and the gate is then just VIX-timing in disguise fitted to a handful of episodes; the VIX-residualization kill helps but cannot fully separate this with so few independent regime observations.

### D1. Per-name effective-spread cost model (Abdi-Ranaldo on free OHLC)

**Mechanism.** A flat 5-10 bps/side charges AAPL and the 480th-ranked S&P name identically. Real effective half-spreads in the S&P 500 range from <1 bp to 10+ bps and widen exactly when reversal signals fire (volatile, stressed names), so flat costs systematically UNDERSTATE costs on the trades the strategy takes most and overstate them on megacaps. High-low range estimators recover effective spreads from daily OHLC alone with ~0.9 cross-sectional correlation to TAQ spreads. Charging realistic per-name, per-day costs (a) makes the net Sharpe honest where it's currently flattered, and (b) enables cost-aware selection: when the book has more candidates than slots, prefer equal-signal names with tighter estimated spreads, a free net-alpha tilt with no signal change.

**Change.** Implement Abdi-Ranaldo (2017) close-high-low spread estimator (a ~15-line pandas function on existing yfinance OHLC), smoothed over 21 days per name. Backtest cost per side = max(estimated half-spread, 1 bp) + existing flat impact allowance. Add estimated spread as a tie-breaker/penalty in candidate selection when position caps bind. Recompute headline and robust-core Sharpe under the new cost model.

**Evidence.** Corwin & Schultz, 'A Simple Way to Estimate Bid-Ask Spreads from Daily High and Low Prices', JF 2012; Abdi & Ranaldo, 'A Simple Estimation of Bid-Ask Spreads from Daily Close, High, and Low Prices', RFS 2017: both validated against TAQ effective spreads. Novy-Marx & Velikov (RFS 2016) use exactly this class of effective-spread measurement to show which anomalies survive; de Groot et al (2012) show cost drag in reversal concentrates in the expensive-to-trade tail.

**Expected impact.** Two-sided and honest: the headline 2.67 net Sharpe likely comes DOWN somewhat (state-dependent spreads bite hardest in 2020/2022-style stress periods that flat bps smooth over), but the robust-core number becomes far more credible, and the cost-aware selection tilt plausibly claws back +0.05-0.15 net. The main value is eliminating the 'is 10bps honest?' question with a per-name empirical answer instead of a guess.

**Kill criteria.** Validate the estimator first: kill if AR-estimated spreads on a spot-check set of S&P names disagree wildly (rank correlation < 0.6) with published/observable quoted spreads for those names. For the selection tilt: kill if it changes > 15% of daily picks (means it's acting as a hidden new signal, not a tie-breaker) or fails to improve net Sharpe under the new cost model itself.

**Skeptic's note.** AR/CS estimators are noisiest exactly on liquid S&P 500 large caps (published cross-sectional correlation ~0.74 overall and materially worse in the top size quintile, with frequent negative estimates truncated to the floor), so within the core universe the 'per-name' cost differentiation, and especially the cost-aware selection tilt, may be ranking on noise or smuggling in a size/liquidity tilt rather than measuring true spreads; plus the implementation must subtract spread from the existing flat 5-10bps rather than stacking on top of it, or costs get double-counted.

### D2. Borrow cost accrual + Alpaca easy-to-borrow gate

**Mechanism.** Shorts are not free even for GC names: fee plus rebate spread runs ~25-80 bps/yr (S3 Partners: 83-84% of US short interest is GC; value-weighted large-cap average fee ~80 bps includes some specials; pure GC ~25-50 bps). Structurally this book is favorably positioned (reversion SHORTS recent winners, and stocks that just rallied are almost never hard-to-borrow, unlike short books that short losers), but the current backtest charges zero, and the occasional non-shortable name in live trading would silently break dollar neutrality. Modeling it converts an unknown into a small known drag and removes a live failure mode.

**Change.** Backtest: charge the short leg a flat 50 bps/yr accrual (daily pro-rata on short gross) as a conservative GC assumption, one line in the PnL. Live: query Alpaca's free asset endpoint 'easy_to_borrow' and 'shortable' flags nightly; exclude non-ETB names from the short candidate list and log how often the gate binds. Rebalance the long leg down when a short is blocked so dollar neutrality holds.

**Evidence.** S3 Partners, 'U.S. Stock Borrow Fees' (https://www.s3partners.com/articles/us-stock-borrow-fees): GC dominance and fee distribution; IBKR short-sale cost documentation (https://www.interactivebrokers.com/en/pricing/short-sale-cost.php); Avellaneda & Lee (2010) themselves flag borrow/rebate as a real drag on the ETF-residual book. Direction of exposure (shorting winners = cheap borrow) is a structural argument, verifiable via the ETB flag hit rate.

**Expected impact.** Deliberately small and honest: 50 bps/yr on ~50% of gross = ~25 bps/yr NAV drag; at this book's vol that is roughly -0.03 to -0.08 Sharpe, i.e., the answer to 'how much does borrow cost the Sharpe' is 'almost nothing for THIS strategy', but now it's a measured claim instead of an assumption, and the ETB gate prevents a real live-trading break. Zero alpha upside; pure robustness.

**Kill criteria.** Not a hypothesis to falsify so much as a measurement; the gate itself gets killed if it binds on > 5% of short candidates (would mean the ETB flag is noisy for S&P names and needs investigation rather than silent exclusion, since systematic exclusion of one tail would bias the short book).

**Skeptic's note.** Opportunity cost dressed as progress: this is bookkeeping, not alpha, and the residual unmodeled risk it gestures at (a short going special mid-position: fee spikes plus squeeze, the tail a flat 50 bps accrual cannot see) remains unmodeled after the change. The gate itself also cannot be validated historically on free data, so if the Alpaca ETB flag is noisy for S&P names, the >5% kill threshold is the only protection against silently biasing the short book by excluding one tail of it.

### G. Point-in-time universe from archived iShares holdings + delisting penalty bound

**Mechanism.** Not an alpha source, a measurement fix that gates proposal 1. Survivorship bias is largest exactly where the expansion goes: small caps delist (bankruptcy, distressed acquisition) at several times the large-cap rate, and the delisted names are disproportionately crashed 'losers' the strategy would have bought and held into a -100%. Current-membership backtests silently delete these, mechanically inflating measured reversion. Shumway (1997, JF) showed performance-related delistings average roughly -30% delisting returns missing from standard datasets. Free fix: iShares publishes IVV/IJH/IJR full holdings with a date-selectable download URL going back to ~2007 (plus Wayback Machine snapshots), which covers the entire 2018-present backtest for all three indices, a true point-in-time membership source that, unlike Wikipedia revision-scraping, works for the 400 and 600 too.

**Change.** (1) Build monthly PIT membership for the 500/400/600 sleeves from archived iShares holdings files (IVV/IJH/IJR), replacing the Wikipedia-based PIT layer and extending it to mid/small sleeves. (2) Delisting bound: any held name whose yfinance price series terminates while the position is open gets a stress assumption: longs booked at -30% from last close (Shumway), shorts booked at last close (conservative, forgoes the windfall). Report the backtest both ways; the gap IS the survivorship uncertainty band, quoted alongside the Sharpe forever after.

**Evidence.** Shumway 1997 'The Delisting Bias in CRSP Data', JF 52(1); practitioner method writeups: Koker 2019 (https://teddykoker.com/2019/05/creating-a-survivorship-bias-free-sp-500-dataset-with-python/) and riazarbi (https://riazarbi.github.io/quant/backtesting-sp500-constituent-history/): the latter documents the iShares dated-holdings download approach; iShares discloses holdings with selectable historical dates back to ~2007. Caveat: ETF holdings lag index changes by days and include small cash/futures lines, fine at monthly granularity, and index-tracking error is negligible versus the bias being fixed.

**Expected impact.** Zero expected Sharpe gain; expected Sharpe TRUTH gain. For the S&P 500 book the existing PIT work suggests a small haircut (2.67→2.50 already observed). For the 600 sleeve the haircut is the whole question: literature and CRSP-based studies imply small-cap reversal backtests on survivor-only data can overstate returns materially, so this determines whether proposal 1's edge is real. Also permanently upgrades the 'known unfixable bias' from unknown-sign-and-size to a quoted bound, which strengthens every deflated-Sharpe claim the project makes.

**Kill criteria.** This one kills OTHER proposals rather than itself: pre-register that if PIT-holdings membership + the -30% delisting penalty cuts any sleeve's net Sharpe by more than 0.4, that sleeve is not deployed. Self-kill criterion for the infrastructure: if for the S&P 500 sleeve the holdings-based PIT result differs from the existing Wikipedia-PIT result by <0.05 Sharpe, the new membership layer adds nothing there and effort stops after the 400/600 build.

**Skeptic's note.** The "quoted bound" claim is overstated on free data, in two ways. First, the penalty only fires when a yfinance price series terminates mid-position, but Yahoo largely removes delisted companies' history from the free tier entirely (delisted-company data is a Premium Plus feature). So many PIT-confirmed members that later delisted will have NO price series at all: they never enter the backtest, no penalty applies, and the worst losers stay invisible. The measured gap is therefore a one-sided LOWER bound on the survivorship bias, not the bias; the build must count PIT members with missing/absent price data per sleeve and quote that coverage shortfall next to the band, or the band gives false comfort. Second, -30% is Shumway's NYSE/AMEX number; Shumway & Warther (1999) find ~-55% for Nasdaq performance-related delistings, and the S&P 600 is Nasdaq-heavy, so the penalty is too lenient precisely in the sleeve the fix exists to police. Run the stress at -55% (or both) for the small-cap sleeve. Neither issue refutes the proposal; both narrow what it can honestly claim.
